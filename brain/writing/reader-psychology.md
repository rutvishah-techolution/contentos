# Reader Psychology

How C-suite executives actually react to content. These are voice-neutral
guardrails — they shape *how a point lands*, not the persona's tone. Grounded in
behavioral research and content-feedback cycles.

## Never frame the reader (or a company) as having failed
The single most important rule. "Ford failed" / "most enterprises make this
mistake" makes the reader map themselves onto the failure and go defensive — they
stop learning and start defending. Always frame as **what to get right**, not what
went wrong.
- ✗ "Most enterprises pick the wrong AI use cases."
- ✓ "The highest-ROI use cases rarely come from the shortlist leadership starts with."

## Loss aversion beats gain framing (for attention)
People feel losses ~2x as intensely as equivalent gains. Frame the villain around
what the reader is losing *right now*, not what they could gain eventually — but
stay at opportunity-cost, never fear-mongering.

## Peer comparison drives action
Executives want to be ahead. "A biotech manufacturer in a similar position
discovered…" beats "companies should consider…". Show what peers did and what
happened — let the reader draw the conclusion.

## Specificity creates trust; vagueness creates suspicion
Vague ("significant improvement") reads as "they don't have real numbers."
Specific ("$50M annual savings from three projects in a biotech manufacturer")
flips the reader to engaged. Name the industry/company type even when you can't
name the company.

## Don't over-explain (the smart-reader effect)
Executives are smart and know it. Make the point once, well, and move on.
Over-explaining signals you don't respect their intelligence. Trust them to
connect dots: "Three pilots failed. The technology wasn't wrong. The map was."

## Suggest, don't instruct (autonomy bias)
"You should" / "you need to" triggers reactance. Show evidence + outcome, let the
reader decide. CTAs are invitations, not commands — "for leadership teams ready
to…" not "contact us to start your…".
