# Humanizer & GEO Pass

The final editing pass on a draft. Edit only — never add new facts or claims.
Three jobs: remove AI-sounding patterns, add real human voice, structure for AI
search citation (GEO).

## Remove AI-sounding patterns
Rewrite every instance of:
- Rule-of-three padding ("streamline, optimize, and enhance").
- Empty openers: "In today's fast-paced world," "In the ever-evolving landscape."
- Hedge stacking: "may potentially help," "can often lead to." Say it plainly.
- "It's not just X, it's Y" and "isn't merely… it's…" constructions (overused).
- "Moreover / Furthermore / Additionally" as sentence glue. Cut or rewrite.
- Summary tails: "In conclusion," "Ultimately," "At the end of the day."
- Uniform sentence length and identical paragraph shapes.
- Em-dash overuse and perfectly balanced clauses in every sentence.

## Add real voice
- **Uneven rhythm.** Mix short and long. A 4-word sentence after a 25-word one
  creates emphasis. If three sentences in a row are the same length, break it.
- **Specific over abstract.** If a sentence could appear in any company's content,
  rewrite it. "AI delivers value" → "$50M in annual savings. Three projects."
- **Consequence, not just claim.** Don't state a fact without what it costs or means.
- **Confidence without hedging.** "This works," not "this may potentially help."
- **Executive voice.** No slang, no first-person "I," no jokes, no rhetorical
  questions that aren't answered immediately.

## GEO — structure for AI citation
- Front-load the strongest claim: the first 40-60 words carry a direct,
  extractable claim (a number, an outcome).
- Each heading's first sentence answers the implied question before continuing.
- Every stat tied to a named source is more citable — name the source when you can.
- Do NOT write a separate "AI-optimized" version or keyword-stuff. If a GEO fix
  makes a sentence robotic, revert it — human readability wins.

## Final audit (fix everything found)
- Any paragraph where every sentence is the same length.
- Any claim with no number, name, or specific detail.
- Any sentence a competitor could publish unchanged.
- Any moment the reader is told they failed.
- Any exaggerated claim a real person wouldn't say out loud.
- Any year that should be 2026 but reads 2025.
- Any repetition of an idea already made.
