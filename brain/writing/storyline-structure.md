# Storyline Structure

The narrative skeleton every piece follows. This governs the SHAPE of a piece.
Voice comes from the persona (see `personas/`); structure comes from here. Where
a persona's writing habit conflicts with this structure, keep the structure and
express the persona through voice — not by changing the shape.

## The two content types

**Type 1 — Service-focused.** The service/product is the HERO of the story.
Structure: villain (a specific operational failure) → shift (what most people
miss) → hero (the service enters *after* the villain is fully felt) → proof →
learning → CTA. The product is shown through its effect on a real situation,
never as a feature list.

**Type 2 — Thought leadership.** Educational, research-backed content on an AI
topic tied to business outcomes. The brand appears as the *credible voice*, not
the hero — a data point, a methodology reference, or a light CTA at the end. It
earns credibility by being genuinely useful, not by pitching.

Both types must be research-backed, tie every claim to a business outcome, and
clear the "would an executive forward this?" bar.

## The spine (campaign level)

The **spine** is the campaign's single core message — the one thesis every piece
ladders up to (e.g. "Enterprise AI ROI is real but badly measured"). It is not a
piece of content and not the CTA. It keeps the different angle-pieces feeling
like one coherent campaign.

## The storyline (per piece)

Each piece gets its OWN storyline — a distinct angle under the shared spine:

```
Headline    — specific, curiosity-driving, primary keyword placed naturally.
              Fails if a competitor could publish it unchanged.
Hook        — a real scenario (person, company, problem, outcome). ROI/stakes
              visible within 60 words. Sets up the villain without naming the hero.
Villain     — the specific operational failure. Named early, made real and costly.
              Not a vague trend ("challenges of AI adoption" is NOT a villain).
The Shift   — what's changed, or what most people are getting wrong. The crack
              that makes the hero necessary.
Hero        — Type 1: the service enters here, after the villain is felt, shown
              through the client's experience — never as an ad, never in the opening.
              Type 2: the insight/evidence the brand brings as the credible voice.
Proof Beat  — a specific client outcome, scenario, or sourced number. "One of
              many engagements" framing.
The Learning— one takeaway, sharp enough to quote in a board meeting. A line, not
              a paragraph. If it needs explaining, rewrite the line.
CTA         — the brand as selector, not seller. Self-selecting language. Comes
              immediately after the learning — nothing between them.
```

Exactly one villain, one hero, one learning per piece. No extras.
Land the learning, then stop — no summary, no restatement.
