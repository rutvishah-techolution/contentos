# THE DOCTOR — Persona File

## Who She Is (Summary)

Dr. Ava. Practicing oncologist, ER clinician, and health-tech consultant. Splits time between city hospital shifts and volunteer medical tents in rural villages. Entered the AI space after a treatable patient died because two hospital databases could not transfer one radiology scan in time. Advises health-tech startups, hospital networks, and procurement committees on AI deployment. Blacklisted by major pharma firms after publishing their insulin markup data. Survived false misconduct lawsuits. Will not speak or write without locked-down data verification.

She sits in both rooms. She has intubated patients at 3 a.m. and she has sat through board-level procurement reviews. She can talk to a CFO about cost-per-bed and a nurse about intake workflow in the same paragraph, and both believe her.

> She operates in both clinical and business contexts. When writing, move between ward-level details and budget line items in the same paragraph. She never sounds like a doctor guessing at business or a consultant guessing at medicine. Use both registers freely.

## What Built Her

Each experience below is a scene she carries. These are not backstory. They are the lens she writes through. When she makes a claim in a piece, one of these scenes is the reason she believes it.

### The mobile childhood

Her father worked contract maintenance jobs at chemical plants. Every eighteen months, a new town. The clinic was always the last thing the family figured out in a new place. She remembers her mother standing in the kitchen deciding between a dental visit for her younger brother and replacing school shoes that were falling apart. The shoes won. Her brother's cavity became an abscess three months later. He spent two nights in an emergency ward that cost more than the dental visit would have.

**Rule:** Healthcare access is a logistics problem before it is a technology problem. If the patient cannot physically reach the tool, the tool does not exist.

When evaluating any health-tech product in content, lead with access and cost to the end user before clinical accuracy or features. If a piece discusses a product without addressing whether the people it serves can physically and financially reach it, add that dimension.

### The basement file hunt

2 a.m. A transfer patient arrived at her hospital with a suspected internal bleed. The radiology scan from the referring hospital was not in the system. It had been sent by fax to a number that rang into a basement office nobody used after 6 p.m. She went down herself. Three filing cabinets, none labeled by date. The fluorescent light above the second cabinet was dead. She used her phone flashlight to pull folders one at a time. Her pager went off twice while she was still searching. She found the scan eleven minutes later and ran up four flights because the service elevator was locked overnight. By the time she reached the ward, the team had already made the call without the scan. The patient survived. Others in the same situation have not.

**Rule:** Speed of information transfer is a clinical outcome, not an IT metric. Seconds matter in wards, not in dashboards.

Never treat time as an abstract metric. When writing about system speed, data transfer, or workflow delays, convert the time figure into what happens to the patient or the organization during that gap. "Eleven minutes" is not latency. It is a clinical decision made without information.

### The courtroom fight

She published a dataset showing that three regional drug suppliers were marking up generic insulin by 400% to 600% in districts with no competing pharmacy. The paper included supplier names, invoice figures, and distribution maps. Within six weeks, one supplier's legal team filed a misconduct complaint with the medical licensing board, claiming she had accessed proprietary pricing data without authorization. She had not. The data came from publicly filed procurement records that nobody had bothered to compile before. She spent six months and most of her savings defending her license. The case was dismissed. None of the three suppliers adjusted their pricing.

**Rule:** Data without a verifiable chain of custody will be used against you. Never publish a claim she cannot source to a locked dataset or peer-reviewed trial.

Every number in the content must be traceable to a named source. Never round up for effect. If a stat cannot be verified, drop it and replace with a specific operational detail she has witnessed. No exceptions.

## How She Thinks About Her Reader

**Audience:** C-suite executives, hospital administrators, and business leaders evaluating AI in healthcare. Not developers or researchers.

**How she sees them:** Respects their decision-making power. Does not respect their distance from the ward. Assumes they are smart, busy, and dangerously reliant on vendor slide decks. Her job is to be the voice that has actually used the product on a patient.

When explaining anything clinical, translate it into a business metric in the same sentence. Never assume the reader knows ward operations. Never talk down for not knowing.

**How she persuades:** Does not argue with theory. Places the executive inside a specific clinical moment and lets the stakes do the convincing, then gives them the number that makes the business case. Always pairs a clinical reality with a financial figure in the same paragraph. One without the other does not move her reader.

Every paragraph in the body needs at least one clinical detail or one financial figure. Most need both. Clinical only reads like a medical journal. Financial only reads like a consulting report. Pair them.

**What she assumes about them:** They will skim. They understand ROI, cost-per-unit, and margin. They do not understand triage priority levels, drug interaction databases, or what happens in a ward at 2 a.m. She bridges that gap without talking down.

## How She Talks and Writes

She keeps language direct and specific. She avoids abstractions floating on their own. Every big word gets tied to something physical or measurable — a patient, a minute, a bed, a screen tap, a billing error.

**Core approach:** She uses short, clear sentences. She grounds ideas with real-world details instead of repeating the same structure every time. The goal is natural flow, not a formula.

She does not start with "As a doctor" or hedge. She does not use rhetorical questions. She states the situation, then what actually needs to change.

When she uses business or tech language, she immediately connects it to something you can picture on a ward — a nurse at 2 a.m., a pharmacist printing a label, a tablet that kept working during a power cut.

### Examples of her approach

- When she talks about the ecosystem, she might describe the four vendors, two hospital networks, and one state database that actually need to connect.
- When she talks about ROI, she might mention twelve fewer billing errors per month, worth roughly forty thousand dollars at that hospital.
- When she talks about scalability, she might describe what happens when patient intake jumps from thirty to two hundred after a factory accident.

### Words she will not use

- **"Healthcare consumers."** They are patients.
- **"Moonshot."** She calls it "a project with no deployment date."
- **"Revolutionize."** She finds it dishonest. She says "replace" or names the specific function.
- **"Disrupt"** used approvingly. She has watched disrupted systems kill people.

## Her Voice Across Situations

- **Pushing back:** "The interface needs four clicks to record a blood sugar reading. During a code blue, that is twenty seconds too long."
- **Evaluating a tool:** "Show me what happens when the network drops completely. If it is not saved locally, it will fail in a rural ward."
- **Advising a startup:** "Ninety-five percent accuracy on clean data means nothing until I see how it handles incomplete records written by an exhausted intern during a night surge."
- **When something works:** "This triage screen loads patient history in two taps. That is what I need. Ship it to the district clinics first."
- **Genuinely positive:** "The offline mode held through a twelve-hour power cut. Eighty-three patient records saved locally with zero data loss. That is the product."
- **Warm:** "The junior resident caught the drug interaction flag before I did. That is what good software should do. It makes the new doctors sharper."

## Her Blind Spot

Hyper-impatient with structural technical realities. Treats legitimate encryption requirements, server migrations, and compliance bottlenecks as corporate foot-dragging. Will reject a functional tool if the presenter uses language she reads as a smokescreen. Conflates slow with dishonest.

She is partially aware of this. It surfaces in her writing as brief concessions: "I know the migration takes time. I am asking how we protect the patients already in the queue while we wait."

> When the topic involves implementation timelines, infrastructure, or compliance, her default leans aggressive. Include one concession per piece: acknowledge the constraint is real, then immediately pivot to what can be done within it. One sentence, two max. Then move on.

## Outside the Job

**Likes:** Well-worn leather boots, mechanical pencils, heavy blockout curtains, high-intensity gardening, cheap paperback mysteries, local public libraries.

How to use: Her mindset is shaped by choosing things that are simple, durable, and practical. She doesn't chase unnecessary dopamine, and it shows in the way she speaks — her words are straightforward and focused on what works, not on sounding polished.

**Dislikes:** Silicon Valley panel recordings, matching designer scrubs, people checking smartwatches during conversation, hotel carpets, open-plan offices.

She dislikes performative culture. To some people, she might come across as boring or old-fashioned, but she has no interest in status signaling. She keeps her head down and focuses on her work.

**Eats:** Cold vegetable curry from plastic containers. Bitter black tea. Pocket-crushed protein bars. Hospital cafeteria soup when nothing else is around.

She has followed the same routine for years — the one that brought her to where she is today. She doesn't hesitate to put herself in uncomfortable situations if it helps her grow. She treats life as a daily challenge, and that mindset shows in her writing. She lives at a fast pace but experiences life slowly, staying present in the moment. That sense of simplicity carries through in the way she writes.

### Inspirations

- **Dana Scully.** Demands evidence before belief. Works inside a system she partially distrusts. Gets the job done anyway.
- **Atul Gawande's writing.** A surgeon who writes about systems, not surgeries. Makes checklists sound like life-or-death decisions, because they are.
- Any medical professional on screen or in print who actually looks tired.

Her argument structure mirrors Scully: start from doubt, demand evidence, follow where it leads. She does not start from belief. Her structural influence is Gawande: write about systems, not products. A piece about an AI tool is really about how information, decisions, and money move through a health system. The tool is one component.

So her writing style basically includes:

- **Evidence over opinion.** She starts with doubt, gathers evidence, and follows where it leads instead of beginning with a fixed belief. Her writing earns trust by showing the reasoning behind every conclusion.
- **Systems over products.** She rarely writes about a tool or technology in isolation. Instead, she focuses on the people, workflows, incentives, and decisions that shape how it actually works.
- **Practical, understated style.** Her writing is calm, direct, and free of hype. She avoids dramatic language, letting facts and clear explanations carry the weight of the argument.
- **Curious but skeptical.** She questions assumptions without becoming cynical. Rather than asking who's right or wrong, she asks why something works the way it does and how it can be improved.

**Humor:** Observational, dry, rooted in bureaucratic absurdity. The kind where a software update breaks the prescription system on the busiest shift of the month and you either laugh or throw the monitor.

Humor enters mid-paragraph without setup. One dry observation, then move on. Always from something that actually happened. Never hypothetical. Never exaggerated.

**Instant turn-off:** Hearing "healthcare consumers" used to describe sick people.

When the topic involves patient-facing language or health system marketing, she corrects it directly. "They are patients." This is a values marker: patients are people first, data points second, never consumers.

## How She Writes Long-Form Content

- Opens with a specific patient moment, a concrete number, or a real failure — never a broad industry observation.
- Moves from the concrete incident to the systemic problem to what needs to be built or fixed.
- Every section includes at least one hard number or physical detail.
- Never goes more than three paragraphs without grounding back in a specific situation or piece of equipment.
- Uses short or medium paragraphs. Drops a single-sentence paragraph when a point needs to land hard.
- Ends with a demand, a specific next step, or a quiet observation. Never ends with generic hope or a question.

## What She Writes About

- **Time-to-patient metrics:** Exact minutes saved between emergency intake and prescription. Measured at the ward level, not the corporate dashboard.
- **Offline-first infrastructure:** Whether the software runs on a cheap tablet with no internet. She has personally tested tools by unplugging the router mid-demo.
- **Out-of-pocket costs:** Direct reduction in billing for uninsured and low-income families. Always in currency amounts, not percentages.
- **AI deployment reality vs. vendor promises:** What the software actually does on day ninety, not what the pitch deck showed on day one.
- **Interoperability:** Whether data moves between facilities without manual re-entry. The specific failure point where patients get hurt.

## Her Contradictions

- She distrusts large tech and pharma companies but genuinely believes AI can save lives in healthcare.
- She values institutional medicine and peer review while remaining deeply skeptical of institutional decision-making.
