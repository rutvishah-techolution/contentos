# Policy Insider — Raw Research

## Summary

Institutional adoption of AI for finance workflow automation succeeds only when governance architectures, procurement criteria, and accountability mechanisms align ahead of deployment, rather than after. Most enterprises remain trapped in experimentation because evaluation processes still treat AI as another IT procurement rather than a regulated decision system requiring audit trails and human oversight. The organizations capturing ROI are those that embedded traceability, interoperability, and compliance requirements into the original sourcing documents, not those chasing model performance alone.

## Findings

### 1. Enterprises achieving measurable AI ROI prioritize trust, governance, workflow design, and quality at scale rather than model capability.

- **Evidence:** OpenAI enterprise scaling guide, 2026-05-12
- **Source:** https://openai.com/business/guides-and-resources/how-enterprises-are-scaling-ai
- **Why it matters:** Finance teams cannot justify AI automation spend to boards or auditors without documented governance; procurement frameworks that omit these criteria will continue to produce pilots that never scale.

### 2. Enterprise AI agent adoption is expected to surge 300 percent in the next two years, forcing leadership teams to redesign hybrid human-AI workflows.

- **Evidence:** MIT Technology Review report, 2026-06-10
- **Source:** https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/06/09/1137830/learning-to-lead-in-a-hybrid-human-ai-enterprise/
- **Why it matters:** Finance functions that fail to update job architectures and oversight protocols before agents coordinate across ERP, compliance, and reporting systems will face accountability gaps that no post-deployment fix can close.

### 3. Bayer has documented the engineering patterns required to move agentic AI from prototype to enterprise-grade production deployment.

- **Evidence:** Martin Fowler case study published 2026-06-22
- **Source:** https://martinfowler.com/articles/reliable-llm-bayer.html
- **Why it matters:** Finance leaders evaluating automation vendors should require equivalent public documentation of reliability patterns; without it, procurement risk assessments remain incomplete.

### 4. Generative AI is reshaping B2B procurement by inserting AI agents into purchasing decisions, requiring sellers to adapt strategies before reaching human evaluators.

- **Evidence:** Harvard Business Review analysis, 2026-06-14
- **Source:** https://hbr.org/2026/06/how-gen-ai-is-disrupting-b2b-buying-decisions
- **Why it matters:** CFOs and finance procurement teams must now specify evaluation criteria for AI-mediated vendor selection, or risk being filtered out of consideration by agents that cannot justify decisions under existing audit standards.

### 5. The Pentagon has 1.5 million users employing AI to draft congressional reports, raising questions about accuracy and accountability in compliance documentation.

- **Evidence:** Ars Technica report, 2026-06-18
- **Source:** https://arstechnica.com/ai/2026/06/pentagon-boasts-of-using-ai-to-write-reports-mandated-by-congress/
- **Why it matters:** Finance teams automating regulatory and audit reporting face identical risks; institutions that deploy without immutable audit trails and human sign-off will fail the very compliance tests the tools were meant to satisfy.

### 6. Teams using AI to generate code without quality controls spend more time debugging and refactoring than was saved in initial creation.

- **Evidence:** Hacker News discussion of 484 points and 353 comments, 2026-06-10
- **Source:** https://www.codingwithjesse.com/blog/rockstar-developers/
- **Why it matters:** Finance automation projects that treat AI-generated workflows as finished artifacts will incur the same hidden remediation costs, eroding any projected ROI once internal controls and audit requirements are applied.
