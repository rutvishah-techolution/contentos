# Scout Research Base
## Overview

Enterprise AI in the finance function is at an inflection point: adoption is nearly universal, but measurable financial return remains uneven. High-value use cases like fraud detection and regulatory reporting are delivering strong ROI, yet the majority of generative AI deployments show no material impact on earnings. The gap is driven by structural barriers—fragmented data, immature processes—and a fast-emerging cost problem, as token consumption in agentic workflows scales faster than budgets or governance. The findings below map where AI is paying off, where costs and risk are mounting, and how the operating model is shifting.

## Where AI Is Delivering Finance ROI

AI is producing measurable returns in financial services, concentrated in specific high-value use cases. Banks report an average 180% ROI on deployed AI applications, with fraud detection and regulatory reporting delivering 350–500% three-year ROI and 3–8 month payback periods ([source](https://thinking.inc/en/industry-service/financial-services-ai-roi/)).

Compliance and regulatory reporting are prime targets. AI is automating adherence to complex regulations including IFRS, GAAP, and SOX, generating automated audit trails, powering reporting tools that increase transparency, and enabling intelligent compliance monitoring that detects irregularities and minimizes financial risk ([source](https://softco.com/guides/ai-in-finance-2026-the-cfo-guide-to-automation-compliance-ap-efficiency/)).

## The ROI Gap and the GenAI Paradox

Despite strong pockets of return, most organizations have not converted AI potential into measurable impact. BCG's AI Radar 2026 found that spending on AI is set to double as a share of revenue, and 94% of organizations plan to continue investing despite uncertain short-term returns; while nearly 90% of CEOs expect AI agents to deliver measurable ROI, few can point to consistent results today. The barriers are structural: fragmented data, inconsistent definitions, and processes not designed for scale ([source](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/the-cfos-ai-agenda-from-automation-to-advantage)).

This disconnect appears across the broader enterprise. McKinsey found that nearly eight out of ten companies have deployed generative AI, but roughly the same percentage report no material impact on earnings—a "GenAI Paradox"—while a 2025 MIT NANDA report found 95% of enterprise GenAI pilots deliver no measurable P&L impact ([source](https://techcrunch.com/video/neas-tiffany-luck-says-enterprises-are-still-figuring-out-their-ai-roi/)).

## The Emerging Cost Problem

A cost surge is reshaping the economics of enterprise AI. Microsoft has disclosed that its AI token and agent costs are exceeding human employee costs for equivalent tasks—a landmark admission from one of the largest AI investors ([source](https://fortune.com/2026/05/22/microsoft-ai-cost-problem-tokens-agents/)).

Unchecked token consumption in agentic workflows is driving the surge. Royal Bank of Canada's CEO reported a 500% jump in token usage in six months, Cisco's CEO called their usage "pretty, pretty crazy," and one corporation reportedly spent $500 million in a single month on Claude AI after failing to set employee usage limits ([source](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/05/the-token-bill-comes-due-inside-the-industry-scramble-to-manage-ais-runaway-costs/)).

Spending varies widely across businesses. In April 2026, AI token costs ranged from a few hundred dollars to hundreds of thousands per month, with a median monthly spend of $2,246 and an average of $140,842 ([source](https://www.kentucky.com/news/business/article316397303.html)). Palo Alto Networks CEO Nikesh Arora stated that token costs must decrease by 90% within two years for enterprise AI to scale realistically ([source](https://www.marketscale.com/industries/software-and-technology/palo-alto-networks-ceo-puts-a-number-on-the-ai-cost-problem-90-token-price-drop-needed)).

## Governance, Risk, and Regulation

AI adoption is outpacing controls in financial services, where AI is spreading as a "quiet insurgency"—employees adopt tools before leadership can impose governance, creating compliance and security risks ([source](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/05/11/1136786/implementing-advanced-ai-technologies-in-finance/)).

Regulators are responding. The U.S. Department of the Treasury released guidance in May 2026 to strengthen cybersecurity and risk management for AI in the financial sector, signaling that AI risk should be embedded in existing risk and compliance frameworks ([source](https://www.grantthornton.com/insights/articles/banking/2026/treasury-guidance-brings-urgency-to-ai-governance)).

## Adoption and the Shifting Operating Model

Integration is now the norm. A 2026 survey found that 87% of financial services organizations have at least partially integrated AI into their operations, with 41% reporting full integration ([source](https://rsmus.com/insights/industries/financial-services/ai-for-financial-services-organizations-in-2026.html)).

The operating model is being redesigned around AI agents. ClickUp cut 22% of its 1,300-person workforce (290 employees) on May 22, 2026, while operating 3,000 internal AI agents—a 3:1 agent-to-employee ratio ([source](https://techcrunch.com/2026/05/25/what-clickups-mass-layoff-tells-us-about-the-future-of-work/)). OpenAI and PwC announced a collaboration on May 4, 2026 to build AI agents for finance workflows including planning, forecasting, procurement, and tax, with PwC building an "AI native finance function" and a procurement agent within OpenAI's finance organization as "client zero" ([source](https://openai.com/index/openai-pwc-finance-collaboration/)).

Architecture choices matter for performance. The strongest teams of AI agents will be built using different, heterogeneous models rather than relying on a single provider ([source](https://hbr.org/2026/06/the-strongest-teams-of-ai-agents-will-be-built-using-different-models)).
