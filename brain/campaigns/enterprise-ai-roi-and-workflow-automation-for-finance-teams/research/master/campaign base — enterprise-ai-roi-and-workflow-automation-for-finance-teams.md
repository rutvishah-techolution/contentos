# Campaign Research Base
## Overview

Enterprise AI spending is accelerating faster than proven returns. The evidence points to a consistent pattern: the firms extracting measurable value are not the ones with the biggest budgets or the most advanced models, but the ones that anchor AI to specific workflows, govern its costs, and measure outcomes at the transaction level. For finance teams — where value must survive audit — the distinction between spending signal and return signal is the central strategic question. The findings below map where value is being lost, where it is being proven, and what separates the two.

## The ROI Gap: Spending Is Not Returns

The heaviest AI adopters are distinguished by spend, not demonstrated payback. Top-1% firms spend roughly $7,449 per employee per month while the median spends $11.38 — about a 680x gap — with power-user spend growing 14.1% month-over-month ([source](https://ramp.com/data/ai-index-june-2026)). That spending has not translated into broad P&L impact: 95% of enterprise generative-AI pilots deliver zero measurable P&L result despite $30-40 billion in enterprise investment, with only about 5% reaching production with measurable value — and the failures are attributed to integration and workflow, not model quality ([source](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/mit-report-95-generative-ai-105412686.html)).

The same gap appears in outcome data. Only 25% of AI initiatives deliver expected ROI and just 16% have scaled enterprise-wide, pointing to an "AI Evaluability Gap" where organizations struggle to measure value and manage risk ([source](https://agility-at-scale.com/ai/strategy/roi-and-success-metrics/)). A parallel root cause: organizations capture less than one-third of expected value from digital investments because they lead with technology capabilities rather than customer needs — a failure mode that maps directly onto bolted-on AI ([source](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/05/11/1136967/fostering-breakthrough-ai-innovation-through-customer-back-engineering/)).

## Where Costs Escape Control

The recoverable losses begin with procurement. Between 30-40% of Microsoft 365 Copilot licenses (at ~$30/user/month) go unused within the first 90 days, and roughly 51% of enterprise SaaS licenses sit idle — wasting an average enterprise approximately $18 million annually ([source](https://www.varisource.com/blog/eliminate-unused-software-licenses-guide)).

Consumption costs compound the problem, because AI cost does not scale linearly with cheap tokens. An orchestrated agentic workflow now costs roughly $1.20 per interaction versus about $0.04 in 2023 — a ~30x jump. Microsoft engineers were found spending $500-$2,000/month on Claude Code tokens, and Uber exhausted its entire 2026 AI coding budget by April ([source](https://thenextweb.com/news/ai-pilled-firms-7500-per-employee-spending)). Quality shortcuts add hidden cost: teams using AI to generate code without quality controls spend more time debugging and refactoring than was saved in initial creation ([source](https://www.codingwithjesse.com/blog/rockstar-developers/)).

## Vendor and Market Risk

Escalating costs and unclear value are already forcing cancellations. Gartner forecasts that over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027, citing rising costs, unclear business value, and inadequate risk controls — and notes that much of what is sold as "agentic" is rebranded chatbots, with only about 130 of thousands of self-described agentic vendors deemed real ([source](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027)).

The vendor ecosystem itself carries structural risk. Analyses estimate over $800 billion in circular financing, where a chip or cloud supplier invests in an AI lab that immediately spends the money buying the supplier's products — inflating apparent demand. Nvidia announced up to $100B into OpenAI, which committed spend to Nvidia chips; OpenAI struck a reported $300B Oracle cloud deal while reportedly on track to lose ~$14B in 2026; and by Q1 2026 investors are demanding "clean" revenue not tied to internal subsidies ([source](https://blockeden.xyz/blog/2026/03/06/ai-circular-financing-loop-vendor-financing/)).

## The ROI That Survives Audit

The returns that hold up are narrow, workflow-level, and time-quantified. Singular Bank built an assistant on ChatGPT and Codex that saves bankers 60-90 minutes per day on meeting prep, portfolio analysis, and follow-up; separately, BBVA scaled ChatGPT Enterprise to 100,000 employees, though measurable ROI is not yet disclosed ([source](https://openai.com/index/singular-bank)).

In banking risk functions, the ROI is well documented. AI fraud detection and regulatory reporting deliver 350-500% three-year ROI with 3-8 month payback periods, while credit scoring achieves 200-350% ROI but requires 12-18 months to reach production due to regulatory requirements. HSBC, working with Google Cloud, monitors 900 million transactions monthly across 40 million accounts, cutting false positives by 60% and detecting two to four times more financial crimes with greater accuracy ([source](https://thinking.inc/en/industry-service/financial-services-ai-roi/)).

## What Separates Winners: Workflow, Governance, and Adoption

The common thread among enterprises achieving measurable ROI is that they prioritize trust, governance, workflow design, and quality at scale rather than model capability ([source](https://openai.com/business/guides-and-resources/how-enterprises-are-scaling-ai)). Bayer has documented the engineering patterns required to move agentic AI from prototype to enterprise-grade production deployment ([source](https://martinfowler.com/articles/reliable-llm-bayer.html)).

Finance functions remain early in this transition. AI adoption in finance reached 56% in 2026, but only 17% of finance teams are actively using AI in core workflows, with 45% still in limited pilot mode ([source](https://www.cfoconnect.eu/resources/reports/state-of-ai-in-finance-2026/)). The pressure to move beyond pilots is intensifying: enterprise AI agent adoption is expected to surge 300 percent in the next two years, forcing leadership teams to redesign hybrid human-AI workflows ([source](https://www.technologyreview.com/2026/06/09/1137830/learning-to-lead-in-a-hybrid-human-ai-enterprise/)). Agents are also entering the buying process itself — generative AI is reshaping B2B procurement by inserting AI agents into purchasing decisions, requiring sellers to adapt strategies before reaching human evaluators ([source](https://hbr.org/2026/06/how-gen-ai-is-disrupting-b2b-buying-decisions)). In compliance-heavy environments, scale raises accountability questions: the Pentagon has 1.5 million users employing AI to draft congressional reports, surfacing concerns about accuracy and accountability in mandated documentation ([source](https://arstechnica.com/ai/2026/06/pentagon-boasts-of-using-ai-to-write-reports-mandated-by-congress/)).
