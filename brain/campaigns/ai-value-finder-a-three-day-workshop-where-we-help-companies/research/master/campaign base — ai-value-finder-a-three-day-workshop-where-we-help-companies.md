# Campaign Research Base
## Overview

The central finding across MIT, McKinsey, RAND, Deloitte, BCG, and IBM in 2025 is consistent: enterprise AI fails at the level of workflow and process, not model quality. Roughly 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots deliver no measurable P&L impact despite $30–40 billion in spend, and the organizations that do capture value share one behavior—they redesign workflows and scope outcomes before selecting tools. This research base organizes the validated findings into the scale of the failure, its root cause, where the returns actually sit, the acquisition and adoption dynamics that separate winners, and realistic time-to-value expectations.

## The Scale of the Failure

95% of enterprise generative AI pilots deliver zero measurable P&L impact against $30–40 billion in spend, with only ~5% achieving rapid revenue acceleration ([source](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/)). This figure is drawn from MIT Project NANDA's "The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025," based on 300+ deployments, 150 interviews, and 350 employee surveys ([source](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/)).: A RAND meta-analysis of 65 enterprise AI initiatives puts the failure rate above 80%—twice the ~40% rate of conventional software—with 33.8% abandoned before production and 28.4% reaching production but failing to deliver value ([source](https://mybusinessfuture.com/en/80-ai-failure-rate-2026-how-rand-and-gartner-expose-the-ai/)).: The value gap is widening. BCG's September 2025 survey of 1,250 respondents found roughly 60% generate no material value from AI while only ~5% create substantial value at scale; S&P Global found 42% of companies abandoned most AI initiatives in 2025, up from 17% the prior year ([source](https://coworker.ai/blog/why-enterprise-ai-fails)).: Deloitte's 2025 survey of 1,854 executives found only about one in five organizations qualify as "AI ROI Leaders," and confirmed abandonment rising from 17% to 42% year over year per S&P Global Market Intelligence ([source](https://www.deloitte.com/global/en/issues/generative-ai/ai-roi-the-paradox-of-rising-investment-and-elusive-returns.html)).

## Root Cause: A Process Problem, Not a Technology Problem

The failure is almost never the model—it is the absence of workflow integration and a defined outcome before build ([source](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/)).: MIT attributes the failure to a "learning gap" in enterprise integration—tools that cannot retain feedback, adapt to context, or fit real workflows stall regardless of model capability. Lead author Aditya Challapally noted generic tools stall because they "don't learn from or adapt to workflows," and executives wrongly blame regulation or model performance ([source](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/)).: The dominant 2025 failure mode is picking the AI use case before understanding the underlying process—a department head selects the most visible or painful work, frames it as "we should use AI to do X," and secures budget for something never scoped ([source](https://mybusinessfuture.com/en/80-ai-failure-rate-2026-how-rand-and-gartner-expose-the-ai/)).: RAND's 2025 meta-analysis of 65 enterprise AI initiatives (2022–2025) found 80.3% fail, with three recurring patterns: poor data readiness, weak organizational maturity, and use-case drift. Winning projects had clean data, clear decision structure, and tightly scoped use cases before starting ([source](https://mybusinessfuture.com/en/80-ai-failure-rate-2026-how-rand-and-gartner-expose-the-ai/)).: Of 25 organizational attributes McKinsey tested, redesigning workflows had the single biggest effect on an organization's ability to see EBIT impact from generative AI; high performers were roughly 3x more likely (55% vs ~20%) to fundamentally redesign workflows when deploying AI ([source](https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/mckinsey/business%20functions/quantumblack/our%20insights/the%20state%20of%20ai/2025/the-state-of-ai-how-organizations-are-rewiring-to-capture-value_final.pdf)).: Organizations reporting significant financial returns are twice as likely to have redesigned workflows before selecting AI tools—inverting the typical "buy tool, then find a use" sequence ([source](https://talyx.ai/insights/enterprise-ai-implementation-failure)).

## Where the Returns Actually Sit: Budgets are misallocated: more than half of GenAI spend goes to sales and marketing, yet the biggest measurable ROI sits in back-office automation—cutting outsourcing, agency costs, and operational overhead ([source](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/)).: Successful back-office automation generates an estimated $2–10 million annually in cost reductions by cutting BPO, agency, and operational costs, while most companies still fund the most visible use cases rather than the ones that pay for themselves ([source](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/)); ([source](https://www.arcastgroup.com/insights/the-roi-of-ai-75-of-projects-fail.-build-a-business-case-that-works)).: Function-level ROI is real and quantifiable where workflows are properly targeted—software engineering and IT report 10–20% cost reductions, and marketing and product development show revenue uplift above 10%; McKinsey also reports 45% saw customer satisfaction improvement and 64% report improved innovation capacity ([source](https://www.libertify.com/interactive-library/mckinsey-state-of-ai-2025-agents-innovation-transformation/)).: In healthcare, administrative AI (billing, scheduling, coding) delivers 200–400% ROI within 12 months and typically breaks even in 4–8 months, while clinical AI takes 24–60 months—demonstrating that process-level automation pays fastest ([source](https://thinking.inc/en/industry-service/healthcare-ai-roi/)).

## Acquisition and Adoption Dynamics: How AI is acquired matters as much as what is acquired—purchasing from specialized vendors and building partnerships succeeds about 67% of the time, while internal builds succeed only one-third as often. The lead author noted "almost everywhere we went, enterprises were trying to build their own tool" ([source](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/)).: Even where AI is deployed, adoption collapses at the user level—85% of employees can use AI tools but only 25% use them regularly (a 61-point gap), and 39% of organizations have no formal plan to drive business value from tools they already bought; 56% of CEOs report zero significant financial benefit, and 48% call adoption a massive disappointment ([source](https://connectedpaths.com/insights/ai-project-failure-statistics/)).

## Time-to-Value Expectations: A three-day roadmap should be positioned as the first step, not the return—production AI that reaches measurable value typically requires 8–18 months. UC Berkeley's Sutardja Center reframes MIT's 95% figure as partly a measurement failure, applying short traditional-software payback timelines to a different technology ([source](https://talyx.ai/insights/enterprise-ai-implementation-failure)).
