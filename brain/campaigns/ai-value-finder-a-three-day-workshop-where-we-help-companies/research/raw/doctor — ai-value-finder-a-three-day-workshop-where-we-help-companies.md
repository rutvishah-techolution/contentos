# Doctor — Raw Research

## Summary

The number every executive keeps quoting is MIT's: 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots deliver zero measurable P&L impact, on $30-40 billion spent. But the diagnosis is what matters for a workshop like AI Value Finder. The failure is almost never the model. It is a broken workflow underneath it — the same reason a radiology scan sits in a basement fax machine while a patient bleeds upstairs. McKinsey's data is unambiguous: of 25 attributes tested, redesigning workflows had the single biggest effect on whether AI touches EBIT, yet roughly 70% of companies skip that step and bolt AI onto processes designed for humans to compensate for dysfunction. A three-day workshop that maps departmental workflows before anyone buys a tool is selling the one thing the winning 5% actually did. My caution to the buyer: mapping is necessary but not sufficient — access to clean data and a defined KPI ladder before build are the other legs of the stool, and the campaign should not promise ROI it cannot chain to a locked baseline.

## Findings

### 1. 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots deliver zero measurable P&L impact, despite $30-40 billion in enterprise spending — and the cause is organizational integration, not model quality.

- **Evidence:** MIT NANDA 'The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025' (July/Aug 2025), based on 150 executive interviews, 350 employee surveys, and 300 public deployments; only ~5% of pilots achieved rapid revenue acceleration.
- **Source:** https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/
- **Why it matters:** This is the headline number every C-suite buyer has already seen, so the campaign has to meet it head-on. The important part for AI Value Finder is the cause: MIT points to the 'learning gap' and flawed enterprise integration, not the technology. That is the exact gap a workflow-mapping workshop is built to close. It reframes the pitch from 'buy AI' to 'fix the process AI has to run inside' — which is honest and defensible.

### 2. Of 25 organizational attributes McKinsey tested, redesigning workflows had the single biggest effect on an organization's ability to see EBIT impact from generative AI.

- **Evidence:** McKinsey 'The State of AI 2025' (March 2025), based on 1,993 respondents across ~105 countries; high performers were roughly 3x (55% vs ~20%) more likely to fundamentally redesign workflows when deploying AI.
- **Source:** https://www.mckinsey.com/~/media/mckinsey/business%20functions/quantumblack/our%20insights/the%20state%20of%20ai/2025/the-state-of-ai-how-organizations-are-rewiring-to-capture-value_final.pdf
- **Why it matters:** This is the strongest single data point behind the campaign. It says the exact activity AI Value Finder sells — mapping and redesigning departmental workflows — is the highest-correlation factor for actually moving the P&L. It lets the campaign make a specific, sourced claim instead of a vendor promise. Position the workshop as delivering the highest-leverage move first, before a dollar goes to any tool.

### 3. Organizations that report significant financial returns are twice as likely to have redesigned workflows before selecting AI tools, inverting the typical 'buy tool, then find a use' sequence.

- **Evidence:** McKinsey 2025, cited alongside RAND's 2024 finding that over 80% of AI projects fail — roughly twice the failure rate of non-AI IT projects.
- **Source:** https://talyx.ai/insights/enterprise-ai-implementation-failure
- **Why it matters:** This is the sequencing argument, and it is the whole thesis of a three-day mapping workshop. In a ward, you triage before you treat — you do not hand a nurse a defibrillator and ask her to find a patient for it. Same logic here: map the workflow, find the bleed, then deploy. The campaign should lead with sequence, because the 70%+ who go tool-first are the failure statistic.

### 4. Most generative AI budgets go to sales and marketing, but the highest returns sit in back-office automation — meaning most companies fund the most visible use cases, not the ones that pay for themselves.

- **Evidence:** MIT 'GenAI Divide' 2025 found more than half of GenAI budgets go to sales and marketing tools while back-office automation produces the highest returns; vendor-sourced tools succeed ~67% of the time vs. ~33% for internal builds.
- **Source:** https://www.arcastgroup.com/insights/the-roi-of-ai-75-of-projects-fail.-build-a-business-case-that-works
- **Why it matters:** This is the finding that justifies mapping every department, not just the flashy ones. A workflow audit surfaces where the money actually leaks — usually the unglamorous back office, the equivalent of intake, billing, and records. The campaign should promise to point executives at the boring, high-ROI functions their instincts and their vendor decks steer them away from.

### 5. In healthcare specifically, administrative AI (billing, scheduling, coding) delivers 200-400% ROI within 12 months and typically breaks even in 4-8 months, while clinical AI takes 24-60 months — proving process-level automation pays fastest.

- **Evidence:** Deloitte Global Health Care Outlook 2025, cited in The Thinking Company's 2026 healthcare AI business case guide; healthcare AI averages ~150% ROI across deployment types with extreme variance by function.
- **Source:** https://thinking.inc/en/industry-service/healthcare-ai-roi/
- **Why it matters:** This is my home ground, and it is the cleanest proof that workflow-first, back-office-first sequencing works. In a hospital running on a 1-1.5% operating margin, an 8-month payback on billing automation is the difference between keeping a rural clinic open and closing it. For any Fortune 500 with a heavy admin function — insurers, health systems, payers — this is the concrete, dollar-anchored case the workshop should model on day one. It also names the payback window honestly instead of promising instant returns.

### 6. AI eligibility verification connected to the scheduling workflow cut denial rates by up to 42%, and organizations that standardized workflows before automating outperformed peers who went technology-first.

- **Evidence:** Experian Health case data cited in McKinsey's January 2026 agentic AI analysis; AHA's 2026 Trailblazers report documented Northwestern Medicine and Genesis Healthcare System standardizing workflows before layering in automation and outperforming.
- **Source:** https://aegishealth.us/blog/5-ways-hospitals-can-use-ai-to-save-money
- **Why it matters:** The 42% figure only happens because the check was moved to the right point in the workflow — at scheduling, not after the visit. That is exactly what mapping finds: not a new tool, but the right insertion point. A denial caught at intake is a claim that never goes out wrong; a denial fought on the back end is staff time and lost revenue. This is the operational detail that makes the workshop's value real to a CFO: named health systems, not theory, proving 'clean up the process, then automate' beats going tool-first.

### 7. Even where companies have deployed AI, adoption collapses at the user level — 85% of employees can use AI tools but only 25% use them regularly, and 39% of organizations have no formal plan to drive business value from tools they already bought.

- **Evidence:** IBM Global CEO Study 2026 (85% capable / 25% regular use, a 61-point gap; 56% of CEOs report zero significant financial benefit) and Writer Enterprise AI Adoption Report 2026 (39% lack a value plan; 48% call adoption a massive disappointment).
- **Source:** https://connectedpaths.com/insights/ai-project-failure-statistics/
- **Why it matters:** A roadmap that ignores the people who have to run it is a scan nobody reads. This is my caution to the campaign: mapping workflows and handing over a roadmap is necessary but not the finish line — adoption and a defined value plan are where 39% of buyers fail after the consultant leaves. The workshop should build the KPI ladder and the human handoff into the deliverable, or it becomes another polished artifact that dies in a drawer. Sell the roadmap, but be honest that execution discipline is the part that pays.

### 8. The failure is a process problem, not a technology problem, and the same three patterns explain nearly every failure: poor data readiness, weak organizational maturity, and use-case drift.

- **Evidence:** RAND Corporation 2025 meta-analysis of 65 documented enterprise AI initiatives (2022-2025) found 80.3% fail; breakdown: 33.8% abandoned before production, 28.4% reach production but miss value, 18.1% never recoup costs. Winning projects had clean data, clear decision structure, and tightly scoped use cases before starting.
- **Source:** https://mybusinessfuture.com/en/80-ai-failure-rate-2026-how-rand-and-gartner-expose-the-ai/
- **Why it matters:** This is the evidence that a mapping workshop's job is to interrupt three specific failure patterns before build, not to pick a model. But it is also the honest constraint I have to flag: if the data isn't clean and reachable, no roadmap saves the project — the same way the best triage protocol fails if the scan is trapped in a system that won't transfer it. The campaign should promise the workshop scopes use cases tightly enough that drift is barely possible, and names data readiness as a precondition it will test, not assume.
