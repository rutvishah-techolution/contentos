# Auditor — Raw Research

## Summary

The campaign is selling exactly what the balance sheets say is missing. MIT NANDA's 2025 study found 95% of enterprise GenAI pilots produced zero measurable P&L impact against $30-40 billion spent, and McKinsey found only about 6% of firms attribute 5%+ EBIT to AI. The root cause is not the model—it is workflow ambiguity, picking use cases before mapping the process underneath, and no defined outcome before build. That is the precise gap a three-day departmental workflow-mapping-to-roadmap workshop fills: the data shows the single strongest correlation with EBIT impact is fundamental workflow redesign, back-office automation delivers the highest ROI while budgets waste themselves on sales and marketing, and the fastest firms cross from pilot to production in 90 days. The one warning for the pitch: buyers have been burned before, so lead with named line-item ROI (10-20% cost cuts in software/IT) and a specific abandonment cost avoided, not the word 'transformation.'

## Findings

### 1. 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots deliver zero measurable P&L impact, against $30-40 billion in spend—the failure is almost never the model, but the absence of workflow integration and a defined outcome before build.

- **Evidence:** MIT Project NANDA, 'The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025'—based on 300+ deployments, 150 interviews, 350 employee surveys; only ~5% of pilots achieved rapid revenue acceleration.
- **Source:** https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/
- **Why it matters:** This is the campaign's entire justification. The workshop sells process mapping before spend—exactly the missing step. When 95% of dollars produce nothing, a CFO doesn't need another tool; they need someone to name the workflow and the outcome before a single license is bought. That is the sale.

### 2. The single strongest correlation with enterprise EBIT impact is fundamental workflow redesign, and high performers are roughly 3x more likely to have redesigned workflows (55% versus ~20% of other firms).

- **Evidence:** McKinsey 'The State of AI in 2025,' survey of 1,993 respondents; high performers 55% redesign workflows vs ~20% for others; only ~6% of firms attribute 5%+ EBIT to AI.
- **Source:** https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai
- **Why it matters:** A workshop titled 'AI Value Finder' that maps departmental workflows is selling the one variable McKinsey ties directly to bottom-line impact. This is the number to lead the pitch with: not 'AI is powerful,' but 'workflow redesign is the strongest measurable predictor of the 5%+ EBIT you are chasing.'

### 3. Budgets are misallocated: more than half of GenAI spend goes to sales and marketing, yet the biggest measurable ROI sits in back-office automation—cutting outsourcing, agency costs, and operational overhead.

- **Evidence:** MIT NANDA 2025 report, reported by Fortune; internal builds succeed ~33% of the time while vendor partnerships succeed ~67%.
- **Source:** https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/
- **Why it matters:** A workflow map across departments surfaces exactly this misallocation—it shows the CFO their money is chasing visible functions while the recoverable cost sits in the back office. That is a specific, defensible line item the workshop can put on the table in three days: reallocate from marketing pilots to reconciliation, procurement, and BPO elimination.

### 4. The dominant 2025 failure mode is picking the AI use case before understanding the process underneath it—a department head selects the most visible or painful work and frames it as 'we should use AI to do X,' then secures budget for something never scoped.

- **Evidence:** RAND meta-analysis of 65 enterprise AI initiatives puts failure above 80%, twice the ~40% rate of conventional software; 33.8% abandoned before production, 28.4% reach production but fail to deliver value.
- **Source:** https://mybusinessfuture.com/en/80-ai-failure-rate-2026-how-rand-and-gartner-expose-the-ai/
- **Why it matters:** This is the exact error the workshop prevents. Mapping the workflow first, then selecting the automation, inverts the failure sequence. The persuasion is straightforward: you are not paying for AI, you are paying to avoid becoming the 80% who scoped backward and abandoned the project mid-build.

### 5. Speed is a measurable competitive lever: top-performing firms move from pilot to full implementation in an average of 90 days, while large enterprises typically take nine months or longer.

- **Evidence:** MIT NANDA 2025 report; Gartner separately reports an average eight-month prototype-to-production cycle and only ~48% of AI projects reaching production at all.
- **Source:** https://mlq.ai/media/quarterly_decks/v0.1_State_of_AI_in_Business_2025_Report.pdf
- **Why it matters:** A three-day workshop that outputs a ready roadmap directly attacks the nine-month enterprise lag. Every month a competitor reaches production first compounds. Framing the workshop as a way to collapse a nine-month cycle toward the 90-day benchmark is a hard, defensible ROI claim—not a vibe.

### 6. Function-level ROI is real and quantifiable where workflows are properly targeted: software engineering and IT report 10-20% cost reductions, and marketing and product development show revenue uplift above 10%.

- **Evidence:** McKinsey 'State of AI in 2025'; also 45% reported customer satisfaction improvement, 64% report improved innovation capacity.
- **Source:** https://www.libertify.com/interactive-library/mckinsey-state-of-ai-2025-agents-innovation-transformation/
- **Why it matters:** This gives the workshop a concrete ROI band to promise per department. A CFO evaluating a three-day engagement wants the return range attached to a function, not a slogan. '10-20% cost reduction in IT if the workflow is mapped correctly' is a number a board can underwrite; 'transformation' is not.

### 7. Cost overruns kill technically successful projects: negligible per-token pricing becomes a total-cost-of-ownership problem when multiplied across thousands of users, turning viable proofs of concept into budget black holes that get abruptly cancelled.

- **Evidence:** Gartner, 'Why Half of GenAI Projects Fail'—at least 50% of GenAI projects abandoned after POC due to poor data, weak risk controls, escalating costs, or unclear business value.
- **Source:** https://www.gartner.com/en/articles/genai-project-failure
- **Why it matters:** This is the unit-economics footnote most workshops skip. A roadmap that maps workflows without modeling cost-per-query at scale is incomplete. The workshop should carry a FinOps line: model the TCO before rollout, or the project dies in production the same way the abandoned 50% did. Sell the roadmap that includes the cost curve, not just the use-case list.

### 8. Buyers have been repeatedly burned, so demand is shifting toward outcome-accountable, vendor-partnered engagements: only about one in five organizations qualify as 'AI ROI Leaders,' and abandonment jumped from 17% to 42% of companies scrapping most initiatives in a single year.

- **Evidence:** Deloitte 2025 survey of 1,854 executives (top 20% = AI ROI Leaders); S&P Global Market Intelligence reported abandonment rising from 17% to 42% year over year.
- **Source:** https://www.deloitte.com/global/en/issues/generative-ai/ai-roi-the-paradox-of-rising-investment-and-elusive-returns.html
- **Why it matters:** The audience for this campaign is skeptical for good reason—they may already be in the 42% who scrapped initiatives. That means the pitch cannot be another optimistic deck. It must open with the abandonment figure, position the workshop as the discipline that separated the 20% ROI Leaders (who tied AI to revenue-focused KPIs and treated it as enterprise transformation), and then show the roadmap as the proof of accountability.
