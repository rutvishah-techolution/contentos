# Policy Insider — Raw Research

## Summary

The campaign has a genuinely strong evidentiary foundation, but it needs to lead with the mechanism, not the promise. The consistent finding across MIT, McKinsey, BCG, and IBM in 2025-2026 is that AI ROI fails at the organizational layer—workflow design, procurement, and governance—not at the model layer. McKinsey's own regression analysis identifies fundamental workflow redesign as the single strongest correlate of EBIT impact, yet only 21% of adopters have done it. That is precisely the gap an AI Value Finder workshop is positioned to close, provided it maps to back-office functions where returns are highest (not the sales-and-marketing use cases that absorb most budgets), and provided it steers Fortune 500 buyers toward vendor partnerships, which succeed roughly twice as often as internal builds. My one caution: a three-day mapping exercise produces a roadmap, not returns—the campaign should be honest that the roadmap is the first defensible step, not the destination, because value shows up on 8-18 month timelines, not in the boardroom demo.

## Findings

### 1. The dominant industry statistic underpinning any AI ROI campaign is MIT's finding that roughly 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots deliver no measurable P&L impact, with only about 5% achieving rapid revenue acceleration.

- **Evidence:** MIT NANDA 'The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025,' based on 150 interviews, a 350-employee survey, and analysis of 300 public deployments; ~$30-40B invested.
- **Source:** https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/
- **Why it matters:** This is the number every C-suite has already seen, so the campaign must engage it directly rather than around it. The persuasive move is not to repeat the failure rate but to explain the mechanism behind it—which is exactly what a workflow-mapping workshop addresses. Lead with the mechanism, not the fear.

### 2. MIT attributes the failure not to model quality but to a 'learning gap' in enterprise integration—tools that cannot retain feedback, adapt to context, or fit real workflows stall regardless of how capable the underlying model is.

- **Evidence:** Report lead author Aditya Challapally: generic tools stall in enterprise use because they 'don't learn from or adapt to workflows'; executives wrongly blame regulation or model performance.
- **Source:** https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/
- **Why it matters:** This is the structural point that justifies the entire premise of a Value Finder. If failure were a technology problem, you'd buy a better model. Because it is a workflow and integration problem, you need to map the institution's actual processes first. This reframes the workshop from a nice-to-have into the prerequisite step—which is the honest and stronger positioning.

### 3. McKinsey's regression analysis found that fundamental workflow redesign has the single largest effect on whether an organization sees EBIT impact from generative AI—out of 25 organizational attributes tested.

- **Evidence:** McKinsey/QuantumBlack 'State of AI' 2025, surveying 1,993 respondents across 105 nations; workflow redesign had the biggest effect on EBIT impact of all attributes tested (Johnson's Relative Weights analysis).
- **Source:** https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai-how-organizations-are-rewiring-to-capture-value
- **Why it matters:** This is the strongest single citation the campaign has, and it should be the centerpiece. It is not a vendor claim or a consultancy's marketing—it is a named regression finding from the survey C-suites already trust. The workshop's core deliverable, workflow mapping by department, aligns precisely with the one variable the data says matters most.

### 4. Only 21% of organizations using generative AI have fundamentally redesigned any of their workflows, meaning the practice most correlated with returns is also the one most companies have not yet done.

- **Evidence:** McKinsey 2025: 21% of gen-AI-using respondents report fundamental workflow redesign; only about 6% qualify as 'AI high performers' attributing 5%+ of EBIT to AI.
- **Source:** https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/quantumblack/our-insights/the-state-of-ai-how-organizations-are-rewiring-to-capture-value
- **Why it matters:** This is the gap the campaign sells into, stated in numbers. A market where the highest-value practice sits at 21% adoption is a market with room for a structured intervention. But note the discipline required: the workshop must actually redesign workflows, not merely catalog use cases. Mapping without redesign lands you in the 79%, not the 21%.

### 5. AI budgets are systematically misallocated: more than half go to sales and marketing tools, while the highest returns sit in back-office automation, where successful implementations generate an estimated $2-10 million annually in cost reductions.

- **Evidence:** MIT NANDA 2025: >50% of gen AI budgets to sales/marketing; biggest ROI in back-office automation—cutting BPO, agency, and operational costs by $2M-$10M per year.
- **Source:** https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/
- **Why it matters:** This is the most actionable finding for a department-by-department mapping exercise. It tells the workshop where to point. A Value Finder that surfaces underfunded back-office opportunities—finance operations, procurement, claims, HR shared services—is aligned with where the evidence says the money actually is, not where the hype directs it.

### 6. For Fortune 500 buyers, how AI is acquired matters as much as what is acquired: purchasing from specialized vendors and building partnerships succeeds about 67% of the time, while internal builds succeed only one-third as often.

- **Evidence:** MIT NANDA 2025: vendor/partnership approach ~67% success vs. roughly 33% for internal builds; lead author noted 'almost everywhere we went, enterprises were trying to build their own tool.'
- **Source:** https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/
- **Why it matters:** This speaks directly to the procurement instinct I trust most. Enterprises default to building because it feels like control, but the data shows partnership beats it nearly two-to-one. A roadmap that includes a build-versus-buy evaluation—with named criteria for auditability, integration, and vendor accountability—is more defensible to a board than a demo deck. This is where the workshop should earn its fee.

### 7. The value gap is widening, not closing: BCG's September 2025 survey of 1,250 respondents found roughly 60% generate no material value from AI while only about 5% create substantial value at scale, and S&P Global found 42% of companies abandoned most AI initiatives in 2025, up from 17% the prior year.

- **Evidence:** BCG 'The Widening AI Value Gap' (Sept 2025), n=1,250; S&P Global Market Intelligence 2025: abandonment rose from 17% to 42% year-over-year.
- **Source:** https://coworker.ai/blog/why-enterprise-ai-fails
- **Why it matters:** The abandonment jump from 17% to 42% is the timeliness argument for the campaign. Organizations are not just failing quietly—they are actively pulling budgets. That creates urgency, but it also raises the stakes for the workshop's credibility: a roadmap sold into a market where nearly half are giving up must be honest about implementation timelines, or it simply adds to the abandonment statistic next year.

### 8. A three-day roadmap should be positioned as the first step, not the return, because production AI that reaches measurable value typically requires 8-18 months, and one credible critique holds that the 95% figure partly reflects premature measurement against traditional software payback expectations.

- **Evidence:** Talyx analysis: AI implementations reaching production 'typically require 8-18 months' to generate returns; UC Berkeley Sutardja Center reframes MIT's stat as partly a measurement failure applying short software timelines to a different technology.
- **Source:** https://talyx.ai/insights/enterprise-ai-implementation-failure
- **Why it matters:** This is the counterargument I would raise before any skeptical board member does. If the campaign implies a three-day workshop produces ROI, it is being right too late—the value materializes over quarters, through implementation the workshop does not itself perform. Stating that dependency explicitly is what separates a defensible position from a sales pitch, and it is the more honest and ultimately more persuasive frame for senior decision-makers.
