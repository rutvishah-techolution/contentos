# Scout Research Base
## Overview
AI adoption has become near-universal across enterprises, yet measurable financial return remains rare. The findings below map a landscape where investment and executive commitment are surging, agentic AI is moving into production, and costs are rising sharply—while the majority of AI projects still fail to deliver on promised ROI, largely due to data and integration gaps rather than model quality.

## Adoption Is Universal, ROI Is Not
AI adoption has reached near-universal levels, with 91% of organizations reporting AI use in at least one capacity in 2026, up from 78% in 2024 ([aibusinessweekly.net](https://aibusinessweekly.net/p/ai-productivity-statistics)). Despite this, only about 6% of organizations using AI in at least one business function are capturing significant enterprise value, against 88% adoption ([unicoconnect.com](https://unicoconnect.com/blogs/ai-statistics-2026)).

A meta-review of 16 research reports found that only 5% of enterprises see substantial AI ROI, with 35% reporting partial returns ([masterofcode.com](https://masterofcode.com/blog/ai-roi)). Between 80–95% of AI projects fail to deliver on promised ROI, mainly due to poor data quality and weak integration rather than model quality ([unicoconnect.com](https://unicoconnect.com/blogs/ai-statistics-2026)). Supporting this, MIT found 95% of generative-AI deployments produced no measurable P&L impact, and RAND puts the AI project failure rate above 80%—usually because of data and integration gaps, not the models themselves ([unicoconnect.com](https://unicoconnect.com/blogs/ai-statistics-2026)).

## Investment and Executive Commitment Are Accelerating
Global corporate AI investment more than doubled in 2025, with total spending projected to exceed $2.02 trillion in 2026—a 36% annual increase—and 86% of organizations planning to increase their AI budgets ([blog.shayaikehassan.com](https://blog.shayaikehassan.com/the-artificial-intelligence-industry-an-in-depth-overview-in-2026)).

Executive commitment underpins this: more than 90% of CEOs plan to continue investing in AI at current or higher levels even if immediate ROI isn't realized in the next year, with nearly all believing AI agents will produce measurable returns in 2026 ([bcg.com](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2026/as-ai-investments-surge-ceos-take-the-lead)).

## Agentic AI Moves Into Operations
Agentic AI—systems that set goals, make decisions, and execute complex multi-step tasks autonomously—is a major trend for 2026, with Gartner projecting that 40% of enterprise applications will use AI agents by year-end ([medium.com](https://medium.com/@aiauthority/5-biggest-ai-trends-taking-over-2026-and-what-they-mean-for-you-0a59b1d8751b)). Enterprises are shifting AI spend toward autonomous multi-step actions, expecting agentic projects to make up over 40% of their AI budgets ([ai-industry-2026.pages.dev](https://ai-industry-2026.pages.dev/)).

This trend is transitioning from experimentation to real-world operations, with 52% of enterprises having actively deployed AI agents as of September 2025 and 39% launching more than 10 agents ([aibusinessweekly.net](https://aibusinessweekly.net/p/ai-productivity-statistics)).

## Rising Costs and the Sovereignty Shift
The cost of AI is escalating, with some companies such as Uber exhausting their entire 2026 AI coding budget in just four months ([forbes.com](https://www.forbes.com/sites/jemmagreen/2026/07/02/ai-costs-more-than-the-people-it-replaced/)). AI model pricing is becoming highly task-dependent, with specialized models and usage-based billing replacing flat rates, leading to potential 30–50% cost increases for enterprises ([forbes.com](https://www.forbes.com/sites/jemmagreen/2026/07/02/ai-costs-more-than-the-people-it-replaced/)).

In parallel, sovereign AI is expanding beyond governments to become a mainstream enterprise strategy, with large organizations wanting to own every critical layer of their AI stack ([forbes.com](https://www.forbes.com/sites/viviantoh/2026/07/20/the-enterprise-ai-reckoning-the-next-phase-of-the-ai-economy/)).

## Where AI Delivers: Structured Work and Marketing
AI productivity gains are largest in structured, measurable work, with studies reporting gains of 14–15% in customer support, 26% in software development, and 50% in marketing output ([hai.stanford.edu](https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2026-ai-index-report/economy)).

In marketing specifically, AI is now a default part of social media workflows, with 46% of marketers using AI for copy and content creation—though an authenticity backlash is reshaping what wins ([blotato.com](https://www.blotato.com/blog/ai-social-media-trends-2026)).
