# Policy Insider — Raw Research

## Summary

The dominant finding on testing AI ROI is not a technology story — it is a measurement-architecture story. Across the enterprise data, roughly 95% of generative AI pilots produce no measurable P&L impact, but the recurring root cause is that organizations buy the technology before deciding how they will capture and evidence its value: no baseline, no pre-registered success criteria, no kill criteria. The same institutional failure appears in the public sector, where the GAO found federal agencies cannot even collect lessons learned from their AI acquisitions and struggle to understand AI costs — meaning the sector spending billions has no systematic way to test whether it worked. The disciplined minority, exemplified by DBS Bank's control-group benchmarking against a stated multi-year target, proves the point in reverse: ROI is defensible only when the evaluation framework is built before the spend, not reconstructed after the board asks. For any executive or public leader, the practical lesson is that a ROI test is a procurement and governance design decision made 18 months early, not a report generated at the end.

## Findings

### 1. Roughly 95% of generative AI pilots deliver zero measurable P&L impact, but the failure is structural — enterprises buy the technology before deciding how they will capture and evidence its value.

- **Evidence:** MIT's Project NANDA 'The GenAI Divide: State of AI in Business 2025' (July 2025), covering 300+ AI initiatives, found 95% saw zero measurable return; the disciplined 5% do the work of baselining, holdouts, and total cost transparency.
- **Source:** https://www.ellvero.com/insights/ai-roi-in-2026-why-95-percent-of-pilots-fail-and-how-to-measure-what-matters
- **Why it matters:** This reframes the ROI question away from 'is the model good' toward the institutional question I care about: was an accountability and measurement architecture designed before procurement. A technically excellent system with no defined success criteria cannot be justified to a board or an auditor — and unjustifiable systems do not survive a budget cycle.

### 2. The core problem in testing AI ROI is that most projects launch with no agreed definition of success and no validation of the ROI they were approved on.

- **Evidence:** A 2025 MIT Sloan study found 61% of enterprise AI projects were approved on projected ROI that nobody went back to validate, and 73% of failed AI projects had no agreed definition of success before work began.
- **Source:** https://www.sthambh.com/blog/ai-roi-measurement-enterprise
- **Why it matters:** This is the private-sector mirror of a procurement failure I have watched inside government: a decision made because the process around it existed, not because the outcome was defined. If you cannot state your success criterion before you sign, you have not commissioned a test — you have commissioned a demo that will later be described as a test.

### 3. Measuring adoption instead of business outcomes is the most common ROI-testing error, and the majority of organizations do not measure returns on technology investment at all.

- **Evidence:** Bank Director's 2025 survey of 141 directors at banks under $100 billion found 82% do not measure ROI on any technology investment; separately, Morgan Stanley found only 21% of S&P 500 companies could cite a measurable AI benefit at all.
- **Source:** https://www.terminal-x.ai/research/ai-roi-in-2026-why-most-enterprise-ai-fails-and-what-actually-works
- **Why it matters:** Logins and hours-logged are the metrics institutions collect because they are easy, not because they are meaningful — the same reason governments once evaluated vendors on features rather than auditability. An ROI test built on usage metrics answers a question no decision-maker actually needs answered.

### 4. The single largest determinant of whether an AI ROI test succeeds is problem selection and time horizon, not model quality — and single-year ROI math systematically produces misleading answers.

- **Evidence:** Analysis finds a 12% productivity gain on a $500,000 process can never justify the build-and-run cost regardless of model performance; realistic payback for transformational AI runs 2-4 years (month 14-28), making one-year ROI calculations unreliable in both directions.
- **Source:** https://www.sthambh.com/blog/ai-roi-measurement-enterprise
- **Why it matters:** This is where my caution about premature judgment applies honestly: a ROI test with too short a window will kill a defensible investment, exactly as a slow institution can strangle a good idea. The discipline is to make the time-horizon assumption explicit and checkable, not to pretend a single number settles the matter.

### 5. The clearest large-scale example of a disciplined, defensible AI ROI test uses control-group benchmarking against a target set years in advance — proving that credible ROI is designed before the spend.

- **Evidence:** DBS Bank reported approximately SGD 1 billion in economic value from AI/ML in FY2025 (up from S$370 million by end-FY2023), against an aspiration stated in 2022 of SGD 1 billion within five years, verified by comparing AI-powered customer outcomes against matched control groups.
- **Source:** https://www.cdotrends.com/story/4914/dbs-banks-billion-dollar-ai-dream-realized
- **Why it matters:** DBS did the unglamorous institutional work — a pre-stated target, a control group, and value traced to revenue, cost, and risk-avoidance lines. That is what makes a number survive scrutiny. It is the operational language I trust: not 'AI is transforming banking,' but a figure an auditor and a control group can both stand behind.

### 6. In the public sector, the ability to test AI ROI is undermined at the root: federal agencies do not systematically collect lessons learned from AI acquisitions and struggle to understand AI-related costs.

- **Evidence:** GAO report GAO-26-107859 (published April 13, 2026) found selected agencies were not systematically collecting lessons learned, that GSA, DOD, DHS, and VA could not comply with OMB's knowledge-sharing rule due to internal policies, and that officials found it hard to understand AI-related costs and lacked access to technical experts to evaluate vendor proposals.
- **Source:** https://www.gao.gov/products/gao-26-107859
- **Why it matters:** This is the finding that matters most through my lens. A government cannot test the ROI of what it cannot cost and will not compare across contracts. The gap here is not technical merit — it is institutional capacity and ownership. Until agencies can collect and share acquisition insights, every public AI ROI claim is an assertion, not a test.

### 7. Public-sector AI increasingly bypasses procurement entirely — through free pilots, donated software, and shadow AI — which removes the very oversight point where ROI and accountability should be tested.

- **Evidence:** The Open Contracting Partnership documented that AI 'sneaks in through side doors' via free pilots, grants, and features in existing tools; a documented case saw Palantir donate software to New Orleans, sidestepping city council debate because no money changed hands. UK government AI contracts nonetheless hit £573 million by August 2025, exceeding all of 2024.
- **Source:** https://www.open-contracting.org/2025/11/10/the-surprising-shifts-in-how-the-public-sector-is-buying-ai-and-what-policymakers-can-do-about-it/
- **Why it matters:** Procurement is the gate where public value, auditability, and cost are supposed to be assessed. When AI enters through a donation or a free trial, there is no evaluation window, no baseline, and no accountability mechanism — the ROI question is never even posed. This is precisely the machinery I watch: the tool that never passes through the checkpoint is the tool no one can ever hold accountable.

### 8. The 'test AI ROI' problem is being amplified by hidden and scattered costs, meaning most organizations underestimate their true AI spend before they even attempt to measure return.

- **Evidence:** Advisors report routinely finding 2 to 4 times more AI spend than the CFO initially believed, with inference costs, GPU spend, vendor contracts, governance overhead, and shadow AI usage scattered across budgets; S&P Global found 42% of companies abandoned most AI projects in 2025, up from 17% the prior year.
- **Source:** https://www.ellvero.com/insights/ai-roi-in-2026-why-95-percent-of-pilots-fail-and-how-to-measure-what-matters
- **Why it matters:** You cannot test a return against a cost you have not fully counted. Total cost transparency is a governance discipline, not an accounting afterthought — and the doubling of abandonment rates in a single year is the sound of budget-review cycles catching up with pilots that were never designed to be measured. The bill is coming due for institutions that mistook activity for evidence.
