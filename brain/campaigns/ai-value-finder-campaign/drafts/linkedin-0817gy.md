# LinkedIn — Draft 2

Microsoft just restricted its own employees from using Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 internally.

The reason wasn't performance. It was data retention — they wanted to know where the data goes before letting anyone run it.

Read that again. The company that sells you AI wouldn't deploy one internally without an audit first.

So here's the question nobody's asking: what is your own AI spend running on that nobody vetted?

Here's what the numbers actually say. 95% of enterprise GenAI pilots deliver no measurable P&L impact (MIT). Not because the models are bad — because nobody grounded the buy in a real workflow first. And the ones that do behave differently: the gap between the 5% that create value and the 95% that don't is process integration, not model quality (MIT). It shows up on the P&L, where 20% of organizations capture 74% of AI's economic value (PwC).

Microsoft's move is the tell. The smart operators now evaluate AI against a specific workflow and its data *before* committing, not after. Tool-first is the old game.

This is exactly the problem Techolution's AI ValueFinder is built for. It starts on the ops floor, not the boardroom — mapping your actual workflows and handing back a board-ready roadmap of 3-5 projects ranked by enterprise ROI. It answers Microsoft's question before you sign: which workflow, what data, what return.

One large biotech manufacturer came in with 30+ scattered AI ideas and several dead pilots. ValueFinder filtered them to 5 board-ready projects. Three went to deployment — an 11-day compliance handoff made autonomous, a 4-person manual reconciliation replaced. $50M+ in annual savings. The high-return workflows weren't the ones on the shortlist: back-office document and service work returned $2–10M a year while the marketing pilots stalled (MIT).

I'll flag what I can't verify — I don't know what's inside your stack. But I know the shape of the mistake, and it's the same one every time.

Microsoft didn't ask if the AI was good. They asked where the data went and whether the workflow was worth it.

That's the whole game.

For leadership teams that have bought enough AI tools and are ready to find where the money actually is — before the next contract, not after.
