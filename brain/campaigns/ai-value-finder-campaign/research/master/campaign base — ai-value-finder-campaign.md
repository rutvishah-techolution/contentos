# Campaign Research Base
## Overview

Enterprise AI spending has scaled sharply, but returns have not followed. The prevailing pattern is investment without measurable business impact, driven by misallocated budgets, weak process integration, underestimated operating costs, and immature governance. The findings below establish where AI value is actually being captured, why most programs fail to capture it, what production deployments look like when they succeed, and which structural risks — cost, governance, and security — determine whether AI reaches durable production use. Together they define the case for grounding an AI roadmap in a company's own operational reality rather than in tool acquisition.

## The Return Gap: Investment Without Measurable Impact

Most senior leaders cannot demonstrate a return on their AI spend. In PwC's 29th Global CEO Survey of 4,454 CEOs across 95 countries, only 12% reported that AI delivered both cost and revenue benefits, while 56% reported it produced neither ([source](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/news-room/press-releases/2026/pwc-2026-global-ceo-survey.html)). The pattern holds at the level of individual deployments: of $30–40 billion in enterprise GenAI investment, roughly 95% of pilots delivered no measurable P&L impact, and only 5% created significant value ([source](https://virtualizationreview.com/articles/2025/08/19/mit-report-finds-most-ai-business-investments-fail-reveals-genai-divide.aspx)).

The spending gap persists even as budgets climb. 59% of companies now spend over $1 million annually on AI technology, yet 79% of organizations face challenges in AI adoption — a double-digit increase from 2025 ([source](https://www.marketscale.com/industries/software-and-technology/enterprise-ai-moves-from-pilot-to-production-in-2026-but-gaps-in-governance-and-talent-persist/)). Value is highly concentrated: 74% of AI's economic value is captured by just 20% of organizations ([source](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/news-room/press-releases/2026/pwc-2026-ai-performance-study.html)).

## What Separates the Winners: Process Integration Over Tools

The difference between the 5% that create value and the 95% that do not is tight integration into real business processes, not model quality ([source](https://virtualizationreview.com/articles/2025/08/19/mit-report-finds-most-ai-business-investments-fail-reveals-genai-divide.aspx)). PwC's AI Performance Study of 1,217 senior executives across 25 sectors reinforces this: the leaders capturing outsized value use AI as a catalyst for reinvention and redesign, rather than bolting on more AI tools ([source](https://www.pwc.com/gx/en/news-room/press-releases/2026/pwc-2026-ai-performance-study.html)).

Budget is often aimed at the wrong functions. MIT's data shows companies misallocate GenAI spend toward visible sales and marketing functions, while back-office automation like customer service and document review delivers higher returns — with case studies showing $2–10M in annual savings by replacing outsourced support, and 30% reductions in external agency spend ([source](https://www.legal.io/blog/5719519/MIT-Report-Finds-95-of-AI-Pilots-Fail-to-Deliver-ROI-Exposing-GenAI-Divide)). This misallocation extends to software generally: roughly 53% of all SaaS licenses go unused or underused, wasting up to $80.6M annually at large enterprises, and IT controls only 15% of SaaS spend — meaning most purchasing happens with no central usage oversight ([source](https://zylo.com/blog/shelfware)).

## Production Deployments That Delivered

Where AI is embedded in a specific, high-value workflow, it moves from pilot to production even in regulated environments. Travelers deployed an AI-powered Claim Assistant built with OpenAI nationally as of February 2026; 85–90% of eligible customers now complete claims through the assistant, following a prior $1.5 billion technology investment ([source](https://openai.com/index/travelers)). In healthcare, Boston Children's Hospital used OpenAI technology to diagnose over 40 rare disease cases, reducing operational burden and improving patient care ([source](https://openai.com/index/boston-childrens-hospital)). Both cases target a defined operational process rather than a general-purpose tool rollout.

## The Hidden Operating Cost of AI at Scale

The economics of running AI in production are heavier than most budgets assume. AI inference now consumes 80–90% of total compute dollars over a model's lifecycle — a 15–20x multiplier over training cost — and has grown from 33% of AI infrastructure spend in 2023 to 55% in early 2026 ([source](https://byteiota.com/ai-inference-costs-2026-the-hidden-15-20x-gpu-crisis/)). AI-first SaaS companies now spend 40–50% of revenue on model hosting and inference, compressing gross margins to 25–60% versus the 75–85% investors have priced into software for two decades ([source](https://valueaddvc.com/blog/the-true-cost-of-running-an-ai-product-in-2026-gpu-api-and-inference-bills)). At the most aggressive adopters, spend reaches roughly $7,500 per employee per month on AI tools and infrastructure — approaching an engineer's salary ([source](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/10/ai-pilled-firms-spend-7500-per-employee-each-month-on-ai/)). These figures make ROI dependent on selecting the right workflows before committing to production-scale inference.

## Governance, Security, and the Agentic Frontier

Governance infrastructure lags deployment. Only one in five companies maintains a mature governance model for autonomous AI agents, with orchestration complexity and observability deficiencies as primary bottlenecks ([source](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06545)). The market is racing to close this gap: Coralogix raised $200 million in June 2026 to build monitoring infrastructure specifically for AI agents in production, reaching a $1.6 billion valuation ([source](https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/03/coralogix-raises-200m-in-race-to-build-the-monitoring-layer-for-ai-agents/)). New architectures are emerging to enforce control — the Queen-Bee architecture, proposed in June 2026, is a governed multi-agent system that enforces policy, tenant isolation, and operational boundaries for enterprise deployments ([source](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.06545)). Researchers have also published a formal framework and taxonomy for managing catastrophic AI loss-of-control incidents, addressing a gap in response planning beyond prevention ([source](https://arxiv.org/abs/2605.30406)).

Data governance concerns are already shaping vendor decisions at the largest firms: Microsoft restricted its own employees from using Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 internally due to new data retention requirements ([source](https://www.theverge.com/report/947575/microsoft-claude-fable-5-restricted-internally)). External threats compound the picture — OpenAI documented PRC-linked influence operations using AI to target US AI policy debates, data center narratives, and tariff discussions in a June 2026 report ([source](https://openai.com/index/prc-linked-influence-operations-ai-debates)).
