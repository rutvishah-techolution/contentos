# Blog Storyline — AI Reached Production in a Hospital — Because It Targeted One Workflow, Not Everything

**Author:** Doctor  ·  **Angle:** The outcome/case

**Hook:** A rare-disease case can take years to name. A child cycles through specialists, scans, and second opinions while the file grows and the diagnosis does not. That delay is not just clinical. It is bed-days, repeat imaging, and staff hours spent chasing a moving target. Now hold that against the number most boards are staring at: 95% of enterprise AI pilots never touch the P&L.
**Villain:** The scatter approach. Leadership funds AI everywhere at once — dashboards, chatbots, thirty ideas chasing visibility in sales and marketing — and nothing reaches the ward. Of $30–40 billion in enterprise GenAI spend, roughly 95% of pilots produced no measurable impact. Meanwhile 59% of companies now spend over $1M a year on AI, and 79% still hit adoption walls. The money moves. The patient does not.
**Shift:** The 5% that create value did not buy better models. They embedded AI into one real process. MIT's data is blunt: the difference is tight integration into an actual workflow, not model quality. And the budget is usually pointed at the wrong door — visible sales functions instead of back-office work like document review, where the returns actually sit. The question was never 'what can AI do.' It was 'which one workflow, first.'
**Hero:** AI ValueFinder answers that question before a dollar of inference is committed. It is a 3-day engagement that starts on the operations floor — not the boardroom — and reads a company's own workflows to find where AI pays back. The output is a board-ready roadmap: 3 to 5 projects ranked by enterprise ROI, each tied to a process leadership already knows is bleeding time and money. Not a tool to install. A map of where the tool belongs.
**Proof:** Boston Children's Hospital used OpenAI technology to diagnose over 40 rare disease cases — one defined clinical workflow, not a hospital-wide rollout, and it reached production in a regulated environment where most AI stalls. The same pattern holds outside medicine: Travelers deployed an AI Claim Assistant nationally, and 85–90% of eligible customers now complete claims through it. In one of many engagements, a biotech manufacturer walked in with 30+ scattered AI ideas and several failed pilots; ValueFinder filtered them to 5 board-ready projects in 3 days, and the three that deployed surfaced $70M+ in identified ROI.
**Learning:** AI reaches production when it is aimed at one workflow, not everything.
**CTA:** For leadership teams sitting on a dozen AI ideas and no clear first move — three days on your operations floor turns the pile into a ranked map.
